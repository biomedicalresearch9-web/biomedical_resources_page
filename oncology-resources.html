Section 3
Career exploration
From surgical practice to translational research — oncology spans a broad range of scientific and clinical careers.
Clinical careers
  • Medical Oncologist
    Treats cancer with chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy
  • Surgical Oncologist
    Removes tumors and performs biopsies
  • Radiation Oncologist
    Uses radiation to treat and shrink tumors
  • Hematologist-Oncologist
    Specializes in blood cancers: leukemia, lymphoma, myeloma
  • Oncology Nurse Practitioner
    Advanced practice in cancer care and patient management
  • Palliative Care Specialist
    Focuses on quality of life for cancer patients
Research & science careers
  • Cancer Researcher / Biologist
    Studies tumor biology, genetics, and drug targets
  • Physician-Scientist (MD/PhD)
    Conducts lab research alongside clinical practice
  • Cancer Immunologist
    Develops immunotherapies and studies immune-tumor interactions
  • Cancer Epidemiologist
    Tracks cancer incidence, risk factors, and trends
  • Bioinformatician / Computational Oncologist
    Analyzes cancer genomic datasets and builds predictive models
  • Pharmaceutical Scientist
    Develops and tests new cancer therapeutics
Education pathway
Bachelor's (4 yrs)→ MD or PhD (4 yrs)→ Residency (3–5 yrs)→ Oncology Fellowship (2–3 yrs)
Clinical track: Internal Medicine or Surgery residency, then oncology fellowship. Sub-specialty fellowships (e.g. hematology-oncology) may extend training.
Research track: PhD in cancer biology, biochemistry, or genetics, followed by a postdoctoral fellowship. MD/PhD programs combine both paths.
